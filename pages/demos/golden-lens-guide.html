What Is Worldtext?

Worldtext is text that occupies space. Instead of scrolling down a page, you walk through rooms whose walls are made of language. Every word exists at a coordinate. Some words glow — they are hyperportals that transport you to another part of the text.

The Core Principle: A word on a wall in Zone A that belongs to Zone B is a portal. Click it and you travel there. Words that belong to the zone you're already in appear dim — they're context, not exits.

The system has three layers:

1
Lore — the raw text written on the walls. Long-form prose, poetry, field notes, transcripts. Each zone has its own corpus. Pretext wraps it perfectly at any wall width.
2
Scene Graph — the network of hyperportals. Each node has a word, a home zone, connections to other words, and an investigative clue. The graph determines what glows and where clicking takes you.
3
The Map — the spatial layout. Five zones arranged as rooms in a raycaster level. The minimap shows the investigation web: zone clusters, cross-zone connection lines, your position, and the active pursuit pulse.

The Five Zones

Every piece of text lives in one of five zones. Zones are both physical locations and semantic territories:

SHACK · folk technology · oral knowledge CREEK · optics · natural philosophy RIDGE · signals · communication CAVE · darkness · epistemology PUDDLE · petroleum · interference

You can rename these zones, add new ones, or remap them entirely. The default five are designed around the demo mystery (the case of Edgar, the missing hound), but the structure works for any text corpus.

How to Structure Your Text

Step 1: Divide Into Zones

Split your source text into 3–7 thematic territories. Each zone should have its own voice, vocabulary, and concern. Ask: if this text were a landscape, what are the distinct regions? 

Zone "library"   → academic sources, citations, formal language
Zone "field"     → field notes, observations, sensory detail
Zone "workshop"  → technical procedures, measurements, tools
Zone "archive"   → historical documents, dates, provenance
Zone "theory"    → abstractions, models, hypotheses

Step 2: Write Dense, Specific Prose

Wall text should be substantive. Avoid filler. Every sentence should contain at least one proper noun, measurement, material, or specific detail. The text needs to be worth reading up close — it constitutes the physical matter of the world.

Bad: "The cave was very dark and scary."
Good: "The dark in the cave is not like nighttime dark. It has texture. It has weight. It presses. 14-inch span, absolute dark beyond the beam edge."

Step 3: Plant Cross-Zone Vocabulary

The hyperportal system works by finding words in one zone's text that belong to another zone. So deliberately use vocabulary from other zones in your prose. If Zone A mentions a concept from Zone C, that word becomes a clickable portal. 

In the SHACK zone text, mention "cave", "prism", "heliograph"
In the CAVE zone text, mention "antenna", "flashlight", "beam"
In the CREEK zone text, mention "hubcap", "collar", "oil"

The more cross-references you plant, the denser the portal network becomes. But don't force it — the connections should follow the logic of the narrative.

Step 4: Build the Scene Graph

For each important keyword, create a clue node that defines its home zone, its connections to other keywords, and a one-line investigative clue.

Scene Graph JSON Format

The scene graph is a JSON array of clue nodes:

[
  {
    "word": "lantern",          // the keyword that glows on walls
    "zone": "cave",             // home zone (shack|creek|ridge|cave|puddle)
    "connections": [            // which other words this links to
      "flashlight",
      "Stephen Bishop",
      "guide"
    ],
    "clue": "Tallow candle, a length of rope, a ball of twine. Stephen Bishop's equipment."
  },
  {
    "word": "hubcap",
    "zone": "ridge",
    "connections": ["mirror", "beam", "heliograph"],
    "clue": "Chrome hubcap. Focal length 42cm. Who was he signaling?"
  }
]

Rules

word — must appear in the lore text of at least one zone (ideally NOT its home zone) to function as a portal.
zone — one of the five zone keys. The word will appear dim on its home zone's walls, bright on others.
connections — other word values in the graph. Bidirectional by convention.
clue — investigative description. Shown in the scanner output when clicked.

Example: Adapting a Research Paper

1
Split sections into zones. Introduction → "threshold". Literature review → "archive". Methods → "workshop". Results → "field". Discussion → "theory".
2
Upload lore. Paste each section's text into Sheet A with the zone selector. Click INJECT INTO WALLS. The text appears on that zone's walls immediately.
3
Build the graph. Identify 20–50 key terms. For each, decide its home zone and which other terms it connects to. Paste the JSON into Sheet A's scene graph section. Click MERGE INTO SCENE GRAPH.
4
Walk the world. Enter LiDAR mode. The minimap shows the investigation web. Click glowing portals to navigate between sections. The clue journal (Sheet B) tracks your path.

Advanced Tips

LLM-Generated Lore

Pass ?llm_url=http://localhost:11434/api/generate&llm_model=llama3 in the URL to connect an Ollama instance. Press G in LiDAR to generate new zone-specific text live. Archive-type keywords (Newton, Foxfire, Spindletop) also trigger generation when clicked.

The Minimap Investigation Web

The minimap in LiDAR mode now shows the full investigation web: zone clusters as colored dots, cross-zone connections as thread lines, your active pursuit pulsing, and a white progress ring showing how many clues you've collected per zone. Use it to plan your route through the text.

Portal Spatialization

Words only glow as portals on walls where they DON'T belong. "Newton" (creek zone) glows bright on the Shack wall but appears dim on the Creek wall. This makes each wall a departure pad — the bright words are your exits, the dim words are your context.
